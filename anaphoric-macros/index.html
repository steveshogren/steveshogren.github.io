Clojure allows you to write macros that mutate the world.

What does this return? Clojure macros by default are “hygenic”, which means they cannot mutate bindings.

Clojure prevents you from accidentally shadowing a value by throwing such an exception. This means that it is hygenic by default, but it is possible to “break out” and make it unhygenic. Now the 111 makes more sense. Unhygenic macros let you shadow bindings. In this case, we shadowed [z 1] with [z 100].

Why would you ever want to do this? Lets look at a real example.

I find prefix notation to be frustrating when I am writing a lot of arithmetic.

I constantly have to scan my eyes back and forth, and it makes it easy to loose track of an expression. I wanted a syntax that was similar to the threading macro, but where I could place the previous result at arbitraray locations.

This is much easier for my western eyes to read, it replaces the previous “inside out” style with a more natural left to right.
